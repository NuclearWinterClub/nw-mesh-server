# NUCLEAR WINTER — Official Backstory
# BEFORE THE DARK

---

## THE DRIFT

Nobody chose this. That is the part that stays with people longest — not the bombs, not the cold, not the silence after. The part that keeps survivors awake is the memory of how ordinary everything felt on the way down.

It didn't begin with an invasion. It began with a shrug.

Con-men and loudmouths found their way into positions of power, and the people who noticed said nothing, because the lights were still on and the stores were still stocked and the watchdogs were still there — weren't they? — to handle anything that actually mattered. The propaganda was obvious if you looked directly at it, so most people learned not to look directly at it. *These other people.* Whoever they were that month. That year. The reason your life wasn't what it should have been. It was a lie told so many times across so many channels that it stopped feeling like a lie and started feeling like weather — unpleasant, unavoidable, not worth arguing with.

Beneath it, something was moving.

Shadow organizations. Competing interests. Invisible wars waged through proxy conflicts, manufactured scarcity, and information so precisely shaped it no longer resembled manipulation — it resembled reality. The working class was managed. That is the only word for it. Managed. Kept exhausted enough to be distracted, kept divided enough to be harmless, kept just comfortable enough not to organize.

And still, most people did not see it coming. Not because they were foolish. Because they were human, and humans are very good at mistaking stability for safety.

## THE HOLY WAR

It accelerated. As it always does, once the restraints come off.

The invisible wars became visible ones — proxy conflicts that could no longer hold their shape, alliances that fractured under the weight of too many competing agendas. Sterling Prescott Thorne — President of the Western World, architect of its theology, the man who had spent a lifetime engineering consensus — watched his position in the order begin to slip, and made a decision that men like him always eventually make.

He decided to stop playing the game and simply end it.

The Holy War was the public face of it — coordinated, simultaneous strikes framed as defense, as righteousness, as the West finally doing what it had always been willing to do. Ordinary people were committed to it before they understood what they had been committed to. By the time the framing became clear, the machinery was already moving, and machinery that size does not stop because someone changes their mind.

The rest of the world noticed. Alliances formed faster than Thorne's models had predicted. The West — powerful, yes, but not more powerful than everyone else combined — began to lose ground. Slowly at first. Then not slowly.

---

## THE SOLUTION

What followed was not a military calculation. It was something colder than that.

If the West could not hold what it had taken, Thorne would ensure no one remembered it that way. The Nuclear Solution was chosen — not for its destruction, but for its theater. The plan was precise in the way that only the most catastrophically arrogant plans are precise: outbound missiles spoofed on every tracking system, inbound Alliance missiles intercepted and quietly replaced with conventional Western weapons, AI-generated footage of foreign strikes on American cities pushed through every surviving broadcast channel. Clean. Controlled. Deniable, eventually, once the story had time to settle.

Communications were severed. Highways destroyed. Regions fenced behind manufactured radiation boundaries — not nuclear, but *labeled* nuclear, which accomplished the same thing. The isolation was the point. A population that cannot talk to itself cannot organize. Cannot verify. Cannot refuse.

What the West needed was not a war. It needed a silence it could fill.

Certain areas were not simply isolated. Oakland. Parts of San Francisco. Milpitas. These were struck directly — conventional weapons, precise targeting, framed in every after-action report as the removal of resistance infrastructure. They would call it the Cleansing later, when the historical record required a name for it.

What they called it among themselves, in the hours it was happening, has not been recorded.

---

## THE CONTINGENCY

Into that silence — the specific silence of a world that had been made to believe it was already over — something activated.

Not a savior. The distinction matters. A contingency. A response, planned in advance, for a scenario that was never supposed to happen and that several people had spent considerable effort ensuring could happen anyway. Whatever shelter had kept the Operator alive through the worst of it was deep enough, sealed enough, that the world above had no way to reach them — and they had no way to reach the world above except through the narrow channels that still carried signal.

The signal went out on Day 47.

A voice. An address. A single sentence that meant either everything or nothing depending on what you had left.

*Come Find Us.*

What the Operator was, exactly — government contingency, private project, something that had been waiting longer than the war itself — remained unclear to everyone who answered the call. The voice was measured. Intelligent. Competent in a way that felt like it outranked most of what was left standing. The requests were reasonable. The gratitude was real.

Nobody asked too many questions. There was work to do.

And somewhere beneath it all — beneath the rubble and the cold and the manufactured darkness and the long, grinding silence of a world that had been broken on purpose — something was beginning.
